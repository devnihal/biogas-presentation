Problem Statement

  • India generates over 65 million tonnes of food waste annually, with significant contributions from restaurants and canteens
  • Mismanagement of food waste leads to methane emissions, exacerbating climate change
  • Commercial kitchens face rising LPG fuel costs, increasing operational expenses
  • There is an urgent need for sustainable solutions that convert waste into renewable energy

Project Objectives

  • Convert organic kitchen waste into biogas as a clean cooking fuel alternative
  • Assess the technical and economic feasibility for Indian restaurants and canteens
  • Promote sustainable waste management while reducing fossil fuel dependency
  • Explore scalability potential aligned with Indian government policies

What is Biogas?

  • Biogas is a methane-rich combustible gas resulting from anaerobic digestion of organic materials
  • Main components: Methane (55–70%), Carbon dioxide (30–40%), trace hydrogen sulfide and water vapor
  • Applications include cooking fuel, electricity generation, and bio-CNG production
  • Calorific value ranges between 20–25 MJ per cubic meter

Anaerobic Digestion Process

Hydrolysis

Acidogenesis
→
Acetogenesis

Methanogenesis
  • Optimal operating conditions: 35–40°C temperature, pH 6.8–7.2, hydraulic retention time of 25–30 days
  • Outputs include biogas and nutrient-rich digestate (fertiliser)

Biogas Plant Design (India)

  • Common designs include fixed-dome and KVIC floating drum digesters adapted for India
  • Main components: waste feed inlet, digester tank, gas storage dome, hydrogen sulfide scrubber, and slurry outlet
  • Typical scale: 2 to 10 cubic meters of digester volume
  • Constructed using bricks or ferrocement for cost-effectiveness and durability

Feedstock & Biogas Yield

  • Indian restaurant food waste typically contains cooked rice, mixed vegetables, dals, and peels
  • Expected biogas yield: 0.3 to 0.35 m³ per kilogram of food waste
  • Methane content is approximately 60 to 65%
  • Example: 30 kg of waste → ~10 m³ of biogas daily, roughly equivalent to six LPG cylinders worth of energy per month

Economic Analysis (India)

Small (2m³): ₹1.7 lakh, savings ₹20k/year, payback 7.7 years
Medium (6m³): ₹4.5 lakh, savings ₹67k/year, payback 6.3 years
Large (10m³): ₹7 lakh, savings ₹1.13L/year, payback 5.8 years

MNRE Subsidies & Support

  • Central government subsidies of ₹7,500 to ₹25,000 per plant based on capacity
  • Additional 20% subsidy available for North-Eastern and hilly states
  • Turnkey job fees offered for installation support
  • SATAT scheme incentivizes large-scale Compressed Biogas plants with financial assistance

Implementation Process

1
Collect and segregate kitchen food waste carefully
2
Pre-process waste, inoculate with microbes for digestion
3
Maintain anaerobic digestion for 25–30 days to produce biogas
4
Collect, purify, and store biogas for cooking use
5
Apply digestate as nutrient-rich organic fertilizer

Challenges & Mitigation

Irregular food waste supply
→ Staff training and scheduled collection
Potential odour issues
→ Use closed digester designs with proper venting
Urban space constraints
→ Use modular or subterranean digesters
Technical skills shortage
→ Develop training and maintenance programs

Conclusion

Biogas production from food waste is a viable solution for energy needs and waste management. Financially feasible with current government subsidies and LPG savings. Aligns with India's sustainability and circular economy goals.
